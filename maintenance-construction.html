When routine maintenance is no longer enough

Some Building Problems Require a Construction Solution

Routine facility maintenance addresses smaller defects and recurring upkeep. Damaged walls, deteriorated finishes, failed building components and multi-area repair requirements may need a structured construction approach.

When repairs involve demolition, replacement materials, several trades or work across multiple areas, the requirement should be treated as maintenance construction.

RCC-BGM reviews visible conditions, operating restrictions and connected building systems before developing the repair scope.

Responding to identified property conditions

Corrective Repairs for Damaged or Deteriorated Areas

Corrective construction may be isolated to one area or affect several connected building components. The visible damage and its underlying cause should be considered separately.

  • Damaged interior walls and deteriorated ceilings
  • Worn finishes, doors, frames and trim
  • Repeated patch failures and tenant-caused damage
  • Building-system access damage and common-area deterioration
  • Multi-area repair lists from property inspections

HVAC, plumbing, electrical or other system requirements may need to be resolved before construction restoration begins.

Restoring commercial interiors

Wall, Ceiling and Finish Repair Based on Existing Conditions

Commercial interiors can be affected by wear, impact damage, system access, tenant modifications and unresolved facility issues. Material, extent of damage and required access determine the appropriate repair.

Wall repair planning

Review wall material, previous repairs, active moisture or system concerns, finish matching and adjacent occupied areas.

Ceiling repair planning

Consider ceiling type, above-ceiling access, HVAC/MEP work, previous leaks, lighting, fire-protection components and matching materials.

Finish restoration

Painting and surface preparation after repair, carpentry, HVAC or MEP activity, with colour and sheen reviewed for consistency.

Restoring shared building environments

Common-Area Repair Projects Without Losing Building Access

Entrances, lobbies, corridors, shared restrooms, stairwells, employee lounges and tenant transition areas experience repeated use and may require phased corrective construction.

  • Coordinate with building management and affected tenants
  • Divide work into sections where access must remain open
  • Plan barriers, alternate pathways and work-area controls
  • Schedule around customer traffic and active operations

Maintenance construction FAQs

Questions About Commercial Building Repairs

What is maintenance construction?

It is construction-level repair, replacement and restoration work for an existing commercial building when routine maintenance is no longer sufficient.

How is it different from facility maintenance?

Facility maintenance generally covers recurring upkeep and smaller repairs. Maintenance construction handles larger corrective scopes, demolition, replacement and multi-trade coordination.

How is it different from a renovation?

Maintenance construction restores an affected area to an agreed condition, while renovation usually changes layout, use, systems or the wider property.

Can you repair walls and ceilings?

Approved wall and ceiling repairs can be assessed based on material, damage, access, related systems and existing conditions.

Can you restore a tenant space after move-out?

Yes. A scope may include walls, ceilings, doors, finishes, painting and approved restoration items.

Can work be completed in an occupied building?

Work may be phased around occupants, access, noise, temporary closures and approved operating hours.

Can HVAC or MEP restoration be included?

Construction restoration associated with approved HVAC, mechanical, electrical or plumbing work can be coordinated within the defined scope.

How is repair pricing determined?

Pricing depends on condition, materials, access, demolition, trades, phasing, finishes, permits and concealed conditions.
